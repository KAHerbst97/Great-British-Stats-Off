\section{Data Cleaning}\label{data-cleaning}

I combine and clean several sources of data on the Netflix seasons of
\textit{The Great British Bake Off}, including:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Judging data prepared by \href{https://github.com/nathangiusti/BakeOff}{Nathan Giusti}.
  \item Baker demogrpahic (age/occupation) data from the
  \href{https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/bakeoff/index.html}{R \texttt{bakeoff} package} and \href{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/sarahvitvitskiy/great-british-bake-off-results-seasons-1-14}{Kaggle}
  \item Hand-collected 7-day UK viewer counts from
  \href{https://www.barb.co.uk/viewing-data/most-viewed-programmes/}{BARB},
  used to supplement missing viewer data for later seasons.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Giusti's Data}\label{giustis-data}

The technical challenge score is different from the signature and
showstopper scores because it is a ranking rather than an absolute
component score. This creates a scaling issue because the number of
contestants changes across rounds as bakers are eliminated. For example,
first place out of twelve bakers carries more information than first
place out of three bakers, and third place out of three is the worst
performance in that episode even though rank three would be strong early
in the season.

For example,

\begin{itemize}
  \item Round 1: rank 1 out of 12 is very strong.
  \item Round 9: rank 1 out of 3 is still strong, but less informative.
  \item Round 1: rank 6 out of 12 is middle.
  \item Round 9: rank 3 out of 3 is worst.
\end{itemize}

For this reason, I make another variable so that we need not treat the
raw technical rank as directly comparable across rounds. I transform it
into a within-episode relative rank,

\[
\operatorname{tech\_scaled}_{ie}
=
1 - 2\frac{\operatorname{tech}_{ie} - 1}{n_e - 1}.
\]

where \(n_e\) is the number of bakers remaining in episode \(e\). This
maps the technical result to a stable relative scale: \(1\) is best,
\(0\) is middle of the field, and \(-1\) is worst.

To keep the model predictors on a common scale, I also rescale this to
mathc the range of the signature and showstopper totals. Each signature
and showstopper score combines flavor, appearance, and bake components,
so the raw totals range from \(-3\) to \(3\). I then multiply the above
by \(3\) to match

After this signature, showstopper, and technical performance are all
coded on the same \([-3,3]\) scale. Positive values indicate stronger
performance, negative values indicate weaker performance, and zero
corresponds to a neutral or middle performance.
